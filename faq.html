General Questions

What is business incorporation?

Business incorporation is the legal process of creating a corporation or limited liability company (LLC) as a separate legal entity from its owners. This process provides liability protection, tax benefits, and establishes your business as a legitimate entity that can enter contracts, own property, and conduct business operations.

How long does the incorporation process take?

Most incorporations are completed within 5-7 business days. However, the timeline can vary depending on the state, business structure, and any additional services required. We also offer expedited processing for urgent cases, which can reduce the timeline to 1-3 business days for an additional fee.

Do I need a registered agent?

Yes, most states require businesses to have a registered agent - a person or company designated to receive legal documents and official correspondence on behalf of your business. We provide registered agent services as part of our incorporation packages, ensuring you remain compliant with state requirements.

What states can you help me incorporate in?

We can help you incorporate in all 50 states and the District of Columbia. Our team is familiar with the specific requirements and regulations for each state, ensuring your incorporation is handled correctly regardless of where you choose to establish your business.

LLC Formation

What are the benefits of forming an LLC?

LLCs offer several key benefits: limited liability protection for personal assets, tax flexibility (pass-through taxation), simplified management structure, credibility with customers and vendors, and fewer compliance requirements compared to corporations. LLCs are ideal for small businesses and entrepreneurs who want protection with minimal paperwork.

Do I need an Operating Agreement for my LLC?

While not required in all states, we highly recommend having an Operating Agreement. This document outlines the ownership structure, management responsibilities, profit/loss distribution, and operational procedures. It helps prevent disputes and provides clarity on how the LLC will be run, especially important for multi-member LLCs.

Can I be the only member of an LLC?

Yes, you can form a single-member LLC in all states. Single-member LLCs still provide liability protection and are treated as "disregarded entities" for tax purposes, meaning income and expenses flow through to your personal tax return. This makes them popular among solo entrepreneurs and freelancers.

What ongoing requirements does an LLC have?

LLCs have minimal ongoing requirements, which vary by state. Common requirements include filing an annual report, paying annual fees, maintaining a registered agent, and keeping basic business records. Some states also require publication of formation notices. We can help you stay compliant with ongoing requirements through our annual compliance services.

Corporation Formation

What's the difference between C-Corp and S-Corp?

C-Corporations are separate tax entities that pay corporate income tax, while S-Corporations are pass-through entities where income/losses flow to shareholders' personal returns. C-Corps can have unlimited shareholders and multiple stock classes, while S-Corps are limited to 100 shareholders and one stock class. C-Corps are better for raising investment capital, while S-Corps help avoid double taxation.

Do I need a board of directors?

Yes, corporations are required to have a board of directors, even if it's just one person (in most states). The board oversees major business decisions and elects officers to manage day-to-day operations. For small corporations, the same person can serve as director, president, and secretary, simplifying the management structure.

What are corporate bylaws?

Corporate bylaws are internal rules that govern how your corporation operates. They cover topics like shareholder meetings, voting procedures, board responsibilities, officer duties, and other operational matters. Bylaws are required for corporations and help ensure proper governance and compliance with state laws.

Can I convert my LLC to a corporation later?

Yes, you can convert an LLC to a corporation, though the process varies by state. Some states allow direct conversion, while others require dissolving the LLC and forming a new corporation. This decision should be made carefully, considering tax implications and business needs. We can help evaluate whether conversion makes sense for your situation.
